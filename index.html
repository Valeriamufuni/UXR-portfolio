Case Study 01
Acquisition Crisis
Diagnosing a 50% drop-off before registration at NeuroFlow
Quant Data (SQL/Snowflake) User Interviews A/B Testing Journey Mapping
The Problem

NeuroFlow experienced a 50% user drop-off before registration. The team needed to identify exactly where users were leaving and why — to fix a leaking acquisition funnel and improve conversion.

Approach
  • Mapped the full acquisition user flow end-to-end in Figma
  • Used SQL & Snowflake to pull drop-off rates at each step
  • Visualized funnel data in Mixpanel for leadership communication
  • Ran semi-structured interviews with 5 recent registrants
  • Designed 2 new invitation email versions and ran A/B test
Outcomes
50%
Drop-off before registration (baseline)
+20%
New registrations after email redesign
5
User interviews conducted
A/B
Testing validated the winning design

Key Insight: Users dropped off at the invitation email step due to small font, ineffective CTAs, and emotionally flat imagery. Language and visual trust signals mattered as much as the UX flow itself.

Case Study 03
User Persona Handbook
Bringing the company's user universe to life — for everyone, not just designers
Quant + Qual Synthesis Database Analysis Persona Creation Company-wide Resource
The Problem

The entire company — not just designers — lacked a shared understanding of who their users actually were. Without a common reference, teams made assumptions, designs missed the mark, and product decisions lacked user grounding.

Approach
  • Synthesized all existing surveys, qual research & quant data
  • Analyzed company's database for user behavioral patterns
  • Categorized all users into 2 groups: Casual & Medical users
  • Created persona cards: age, gender, use case (Medicaid, insurance benefits, etc.)
  • Added healthcare-specific attributes: diagnoses, severity levels, onsets
Two User Groups
Casual Users
Work & insurance benefits, general wellness, employer-sponsored programs. Lower severity health needs, motivated by convenience and accessibility.
Medical Users
Medicaid, clinical referrals, specific diagnoses & severity levels. Unique onsets, higher vulnerability — needed specialized design consideration.

Impact: The handbook became a company-wide reference used across all teams. Designers reported more confident, user-grounded decisions. First time the company had a unified view of its entire user universe.

Case Study 04
Non-traditional Approach to UXR
Diagnosing workflow friction between Audit & Data teams at Discover Financial Services
Semi-structured Interviews User Personas User Flows Whiteboarding Facilitation
The Problem

Discover Financial Services relied on seamless collaboration between their Audit and Data teams — but frictions in their workflows were surfacing and affecting client outcomes. The goal was to investigate root causes and identify solutions to improve efficiency and cross-team synergy, then prepare RSM leadership to run a whiteboarding session with Discover executives.

Approach
  • Conducted secondary research to understand the audit domain and build credibility with a new team
  • Collaborated with the PM to plan research, scheduling, and interview scripts
  • Ran semi-structured online interviews (~30 min) with 4 audit team members and 4 data team members
  • Created 6 branded user personas — one per key actor (e.g. Audit Lead, Design Lead)
  • Mapped 2 user flows (audit + data analytics), marking collaboration touchpoints and friction points
  • Built a customized FigJam file with diagrams and activities to support whiteboarding facilitation
  • Coached RSM team member to facilitate the session with Discover leadership
Deliverables
6
Branded user personas (1 per actor)
2
End-to-end user flows mapped
8
User interviews conducted
1mo
Full project timeline
Key Challenges
  • Rapidly learning a new domain (financial audit) to build credibility with stakeholders
  • Digesting a large volume of qual data in a very short timeframe
  • Coaching a non-researcher to facilitate a high-stakes executive whiteboarding session
  • Navigating deliverable censorship and consensus-building with a cross-functional PM

What made this non-traditional: This wasn't a typical product UXR engagement — it was enterprise process research inside a financial services firm, with a tight timeline, a new domain, and the added layer of preparing others to present the findings to C-suite leadership. It required adaptability, fast domain learning, and strong stakeholder management.

UX Design Project
Case Study 05 · UX Research & Design
Lando's Liquors & Libations App
Designing a mobile alcohol delivery app from scratch — with a competitor-beating feature born from user research
Competitor Analysis User Interviews Paper Wireframes Figma Prototype Shipped Product
Context

A Texas-based liquor store chain owner wanted to build a mobile app to offer alcohol delivery directly from his stores to customers — competing with apps like Drizly, Gopuff, and Minibar. I led UX research, UX design, and contributed to logo design, working alongside two engineers (front-end and back-end) and the client from concept to beta launch.

Phase 01 — Research
🔍
Competitor Analysis
Downloaded and tested 6 alcohol delivery apps: Drizly, Saucey, Gopuff, DoorDash, Minibar, and Swill. Built a feature comparison matrix across 17 dimensions — delivery time, fees, scheduling, pick-up options, product detail depth, rewards systems, and more.
🎯
Key Gap Found
None of the 6 competitors offered curated alcohol packages for events. All required users to manually browse and select individual items — a significant friction point for party planning scenarios.
💬
User Interviews
Interviews revealed a clear pain point: users struggled to select the right quantity and variety of alcohol for events like birthday parties, boat parties, and BBQs. This directly validated the competitor gap — and sparked the app's standout feature.
Feature Born from Research
"Alcohol Packages" — not offered by any competitor
Because users consistently struggled to plan event alcohol orders, we designed pre-curated packages tailored to specific occasions — Boat Party, Bachelorette, Backyard BBQ, Friends Reunion, Dinner Party. Each package comes with a pre-selected alcohol selection optimized for the event type and number of guests. Users can customize quantities but skip the manual browsing entirely.
Phase 02 — Design
Paper Wireframes

Started with hand-drawn wireframes to map key flows and resolve open design questions with the client before moving to Figma — including navigation architecture, delivery type selection, checkout structure, and the packages flow.

  • Mapped delivery options: ASAP, scheduled, in-store pick-up, curbside
  • Sketched checkout structure with delivery address, payment method, driver tip
  • Documented open stakeholder questions directly on wireframes
  • Explored navigation patterns before committing to bottom nav bar

Checkout & delivery options flow

Figma Prototype — Flows Built
  • Sign in / Sign up with age verification (21+)
  • Bottom navigation architecture (Home, Search, Account, Cart)
  • Item purchase happy flow — browse → product detail → cart → checkout → confirmation
  • "Help me choose" guided discovery feature
  • Alcohol Packages flow — select package type, guests, days, confirm
  • Search flow with intermediate autocomplete state
  • Account flow — profile, favorites, delivery addresses, payment methods
  • Error states — expired card warnings, incorrect address alerts
  • Component variants — button iterations, schedule order variants
  • App icon design — multiple variants presented to client

What this project demonstrates: End-to-end UX ownership — from competitor analysis and user interviews through paper wireframes, Figma prototype, and engineering handoff. The Alcohol Packages feature is a direct example of research driving product innovation: a pain point discovered in interviews became a differentiating feature that none of the market leaders offered.
